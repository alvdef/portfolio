Hi, I'm Álvaro and I like to create things from scratch. Thats the reason why I'm a software engineer, and I think thats why I enjoyed LEGOs when I was a kid or design when I was a teenager. The thing I live for is finding projects to be obsessed with and *learning things by exploring*. 

When I was a teenager I started to tinker with my dad's camera. So naturally, I learnt to edit photos and videos using the Adobe Suite (obviously I paid the license, as everyone should do). Then, one day in school a friend and I got an idea for a streetwear brand. It didn't end in what most people would call "success", but the lessons I took away were completely worth it. And that made it totally worth it.
![[nandit.jpeg|Photos of our SS21 collection]]


In 2021, the year before university, I discovered coding and I fell in love with it. The challenge was as big as you wanted it to be and I wasn't constrained by anything else other than the time spent learning and building. It was like designing but better, the reward came much faster. It was addictive. So I made the obvious decision: I enrolled in Computer Science.

Even though the university was teaching me a lot I started doing online courses to learn more, as I had always done. I learnt web development, which despite not loving it, helped me build the "product mindset". Also, it isn't bound to be some terminal tool or a cool technical experiment, it can be something normal people can use and enjoy. In the meantime I was reselling clothes and sneakers, because why not.

In my second year I was hired for a research assistant position in the university. I was tutored by Javier Fabra (huge thanks to him for everything), and kept working on it through the rest of my studies (also during my Erasmus year). First I worked on a project aimed to integrate the Europass credential system in the university under the Certidigital project. My work was focused on developing a beta version for the system, and it taught me a lot about integration with legacy infrastructure, while I kept improving my web dev skills.

![[ofi.jpg|I took this photo on my first in the office. ]]

After some months I started working on a new project that later became my thesis. The goal was to take advantage of the variability in AWS EC2 Spot Instances prices to allocate capacity when the cost was at the lowest. The system involved developing a complete framework for prediction, composed of a historical data capture module and a generalist predictive model built with PyTorch. This project was a huge learning opportunity for me, as I got to develop a complete data product involving the biggest cloud provider while getting to know its architecture and trade-offs. During the project, I collaborated with Universitat Rovira i Virgili (URV) to integrate the system into their research pipelines, helping them modify their deployments to tolerate cloud interruptions and migrate to EC2 Spot Instances.

Last year, during my Erasmus year in Napoli, I encountered my next great decision: what to do after finishing my bachelor's. I had two things clear:
1. I wanted to live outside of Spain for a few more years.
2. I was loving my thesis, particularly the deep learning research.

So, when the possibility of studying an MSc in Data Science at TU Wien came up, I knew it was the right move. The university is excellent, and I had loved the vibe of the city when I visited earlier that year.

Moving here was also a deliberate shift in environment; Vienna is the antithesis of Napoli. Changing the chaos and improvisation of Napoli for the order and structure of Vienna has been an interesting contrast. Experiencing both extremes has made me appreciate the unique value of each, though if I'm being completely honest, Napoli is still my favourite city of the two.

And here I am, writing this from my room in a Studentwohnheim in the 20th district of Vienna. During this year, I've focused on deepening my knowledge of the rigorous side of data science (like statistical simulation or scientific experiment design), alongside learning German. Both are a personal challenge, as I usually teach myself by building projects out of pure curiosity. Going to class provides the structure I need to improve my language skills and the theoretical foundations of the field.

Over Christmas break, I started using agentic tools like Claude Code, and it gave me the exact same feeling as my very first coding project. The friction is almost gone. Let's keep building.
